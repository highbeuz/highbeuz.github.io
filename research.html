Early Career Research (MSc)

During my early research career at CERAAS (Thiès, Senegal) as an MSc intern, my work focused on identifying contrasting millet (Pennisetum glaucum) accessions for terminal drought tolerance. This research combined detailed phenotypic characterization of drought-related traits with genotypic analyses to distinguish tolerant and sensitive accessions under water-limited conditions.

Building on this experience, I contributed to a large-scale study of sorghum genetic diversity from marginal production environments in Senegal using SSR molecular markers. This work involved extensive field surveys, farmer-led landrace collection, laboratory-based genotyping, and population genetic analyses.

The study provided one of the first insights into the structure and diversity of sorghum landraces cultivated in marginal agroecological zones, supporting sustainable in situ conservation and genetic resource management.

PhD Research – UAV High-Throughput Phenotyping for Drought Tolerance

My PhD research evaluated the potential of UAV-based high-throughput phenotyping to accelerate sorghum breeding under water-limited conditions. Multi-environment field experiments conducted in Senegal under contrasting irrigation regimes integrated UAV-mounted multispectral imagery with ground-based measurements.

Vegetation indices including NDVI, GNDVI, and MSAVI2 showed strong and consistent correlations with field-measured LAI and biomass (R² > 0.80) across growth stages, enabling robust regression-based prediction of drought-related traits.

Cross-environment analyses showed that while UAV phenotyping is scalable and efficient, site-specific model calibration is required when canopy structure, soil background, or management practices differ.

Regression relationships between UAV-derived vegetation indices and LAI/biomass under well-watered and drought-stressed conditions.

UAV-derived plant height estimates were obtained using DSM–DTM approaches and showed strong agreement with ground-based measurements throughout the growing season, demonstrating the robustness of UAV-based structural trait extraction.

Comparison of UAV-derived and ground-measured plant height across days after sowing.

A comparative genome-wide association study (GWAS) using both manually measured and UAV-derived biomass identified identical genomic regions associated with drought tolerance, validating UAV phenotyping as a reliable substitute for traditional methods.

Overall, this work demonstrates that UAV-based phenotyping is a powerful, non-destructive tool for integrating phenomics and genomics to accelerate the selection of drought-resilient sorghum genotypes.

Postdoctoral Research

Exploring the Capacity of UAV and ML to Predict Soil Properties in Sorghum Field

During my postdoctoral research, I worked on a non-destructive framework to predict soil nitrate (SNO₃) and soil organic carbon (SOC) using UAV-based multispectral imagery and machine learning. This study explores how UAV multispectral imagery capturing plant vegetation responses can be used to predict soil organic carbon and nitrate, highlighting the close relationship between crop growth patterns and soil nutrient storage.

This work demonstrates the potential of UAV-based phenotyping to support cost-effective soil health assessment.

Grain Quality Modeling Using UAV and Machine Learning

I led a multi-year (2023–2024) study integrating machine learning, UAV-based phenotyping, physiological measurements, and agronomic management data to model key sorghum grain biochemical traits. Using commercial sorghum hybrids grown under different management practices (precision nitrogen, cover cropping, and no-till), we developed predictive models for crude protein, starch, amylose, lysine, and crude fat with the goal of reducing reliance on costly laboratory analyses. Regularized regression models (LASSO, ELN) achieved high predictive accuracy for protein and amylose, while PLS and Bayesian Ridge performed best for starch and lysine.

The study revealed strong genotype × management × year interactions, highlighting the importance of multi-year data for model generalization. Our results demonstrate the potential of combining UAV-derived indices, physiological traits, and machine learning to support cost-effective grain quality assessment and precision breeding strategies in sorghum.

Hybrid Deep-Supervised Learning for Lodging Detection and Spatial Analysis (Ongoing)

This ongoing project develops a UAV-based high-throughput phenotyping framework to detect, classify, and predict sorghum lodging across environments, addressing the limitations of labor-intensive and subjective field scoring. Multispectral drone imagery, ground phenotypic measurements, and hybrid machine learning models were integrated across multi-location trials in Missouri and South Carolina during the 2023–2024 seasons. Convolutional neural networks were used for feature extraction and coupled with Random Forest and XGBoost models to classify lodging type (root, stem, or non-lodged) with accuracies up to ~91% and to predict lodging severity with moderate but meaningful performance (R² ≈ 0.67). Spatial lodging maps revealed strong genotype-by-environment interactions, highlighting the combined roles of plant architecture and physiological traits in lodging susceptibility.

Overall, this work demonstrates the potential of UAV-enabled hybrid modeling as a scalable and objective tool for lodging phenotyping, supporting the selection of lodging-resilient sorghum genotypes and improved crop management strategies.
